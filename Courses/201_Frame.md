## Before You Begin

Identity tells you who you are.

Frame tells you *how* you're built to express it.

If Identity is the foundation, Frame is the blueprint. It is the specific architecture of your inner life — the combination of passions, experiences, abilities, personality, and expression that makes you capable of doing something no one else can do in exactly the same way.

This course introduces the **FRAME** framework — Joseph's original model for self-discovery. It draws on:

- Rick Warren's purpose-driven gifts framework, adapted and significantly expanded
- **Big Five Personality Psychology** (Costa & McCrae) — the most empirically validated personality model in research
- **Positive Psychology** and VIA Character Strengths (Seligman & Peterson)
- **Strengths-based development** (Gallup CliftonStrengths)
- **Flow Theory** (Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi)

By the end of this course, you will have a written **FRAME Profile** — a working map of how you are specifically designed to contribute.

## Module 1: You Were Built Differently — On Purpose

### The Problem with Generic

Education systems are largely designed for uniformity. Grade everyone on the same scale. Teach the same content. Reward the same kinds of intelligence. Produce graduates who meet a predetermined standard.

That system has its uses. But it has a costly side effect: it trains people to believe that value is uniform — that if you don't fit the standard template, something is wrong with you.

The result is a generation of adults who are fluent in their weaknesses and nearly illiterate about their strengths.

FRAME rejects that premise entirely.

There is no standard template for human contribution. The diversity of human passion, ability, and experience is not a problem to be managed — it is the mechanism by which the world gets better. An orchestra doesn't work if everyone plays the same instrument. A hospital can't function if everyone is a surgeon. A city doesn't run if every person has the same job.

Human civilization works the same way. The world needs entrepreneurs and poets, engineers and counselors, architects and advocates — and everything in between. The question is not whether your particular combination is needed. The question is whether you know what it is.

### Why FRAME Matters

Most people make the two biggest decisions of their lives — what to study and what career to pursue — with almost no knowledge of their own design. They choose based on what pays well, what their parents want, what seems prestigious, or what their peers are doing.

FRAME is the tool that should have come first.

When you understand your Frame — truly understand it — career decisions become clearer. Relationship dynamics make more sense. You understand why certain environments energize you and others drain you. You know what kind of work to pursue and what kind to delegate.

You stop trying to be someone else's version of excellent and start becoming the world-class version of yourself.

### Reflection Questions

1. Growing up, who told you what you were good at? Was their assessment based on your actual gifts, or on their expectations?
2. What is one thing you've been told you're not good at that you suspect was actually a gift in the wrong environment?
3. What do people most frequently come to you for — what do they trust you with — even without being asked?

## Module 2: Fire — Finding Your Fuel

### What Fire Is

Fire is not motivation. Motivation comes and goes with circumstances and reward. Fire is the underlying passion that doesn't require external maintenance — it burns on its own because it is woven into who you are.

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi — Hungarian-American psychologist and author of *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience* — spent decades studying peak human performance. His central finding: people are most creative, most productive, and most fulfilled when doing something that produces a state of complete absorption he called "flow" — where time disappears, effort feels effortless, and performance peaks.

Flow doesn't happen with tasks you're indifferent to. It happens with things you genuinely care about. That is your Fire revealing itself.

### Three Diagnostic Questions

**1. What do you do for free?**
What would you do even if no one paid you, even if no one watched, even if no one cared? Your voluntary investments of time and attention are among the most reliable indicators of your actual passion — because they're not shaped by reward.

**2. What makes you genuinely angry?**
Not petty frustration — deep, sustained anger at a problem, injustice, or failure you can't look away from. Deep passion and deep anger are frequently neighbors. The problems that infuriate you when others seem unbothered are often the ones you were designed to solve.

**3. What did you love before the world told you what to love?**
Before career pressure, academic expectation, or social comparison — what did you gravitate toward naturally as a child? Childhood interests are often unfiltered data about real passion, before performance started distorting the signal.

### Fire Is Energy, Not Just Enjoyment

The practical test for Fire is simple: does this activity *give* you energy, or *take* it? We often accept draining work as "necessary" without questioning whether it's actually necessary — or just familiar.

A nation builder who knows their Fire can orient their work toward it, even within real constraints. You may not rebuild your entire life around passion overnight. But you can begin moving toward it deliberately, one decision at a time.

### A Note on Passion and Purpose

David — warrior-king and one of history's most prolific poets — wrote: *"Delight yourself in the Lord, and he will give you the desires of your heart."* (Psalm 37:4) Set aside theology for a moment and look at the psychology: your deepest desires are not your enemies. They are not something to distrust or suppress. They are often your design revealing itself to you.

Many cultural and religious traditions have trained people to be suspicious of their own passions. FRAME takes a different position: **your passions are data**. They are one of the most reliable indicators of what you were built to contribute. Take them seriously.

### Reflection Questions

1. If money, social judgment, and expectation were completely removed from the equation — what three things would you spend your time on?
2. What problem in the world makes you genuinely angry when others seem indifferent?
3. When were the last three times you completely lost track of time because you were so absorbed in what you were doing?

## Module 3: Roots — Your Story Is Your Strength

### Reframing the Past

There is a common idea in self-help culture: leave your past behind. Move on. Don't let it define you.

That advice is half-right and dangerously half-wrong.

Your past should not imprison you. But it is one of your greatest resources. The experiences that formed you — including, especially, the painful ones — have given you something no formal education can replicate: a perspective earned through actual living. That perspective is an asset, not a liability.

Roots is the R in FRAME. It encompasses three categories of formative experience:

### Category 1: Defining Moments

The events that changed how you see the world. These may be painful — loss, failure, betrayal, adversity — or joyful — achievement, breakthrough, discovery, love. Both carry shaping power. Both leave you with something you didn't have before.

The question is not *what happened* to you. It is *what you learned* — what perspective, resilience, or understanding you now carry because of it.

### Category 2: Formative Relationships

The people who most influenced who you became. Mentors, parents, teachers, rivals, adversaries. Every significant relationship leaves a deposit — sometimes a gift, sometimes a wound that became wisdom once it healed.

Who were the five people who most shaped how you see yourself, the world, and what's possible? What did each one give you?

### Category 3: Cultural Background

Your community, heritage, tradition, and upbringing. These give you a frame of reference that is specific to you — and often more valuable than you realize once you leave that environment. The person who grew up in poverty understands things about resourcefulness that the person who grew up in comfort cannot fully access. The person who grew up navigating multiple cultures understands something about translation and belonging that the person from a single culture may never grasp.

Your background is not a limitation. It is data. It is perspective. It is often the thing that makes your contribution irreplaceable in the rooms you'll eventually walk into.

### Roots in Action — The Joseph Model

Joseph of Egypt — dream interpreter, supply chain strategist, and eventual prime minister who managed the food security of an entire region during seven years of famine — spent years in slavery and wrongful imprisonment before reaching the role he was built for. Every season of hardship gave him something the position required: resilience that couldn't be faked, administrative skill developed under real pressure, and the emotional intelligence that comes only from suffering with grace.

His Roots — every difficult and unjust one — were preparation.

The same pattern appears in nearly every person who has made a significant contribution to the world. What they survived taught them what no classroom could: what it costs to lose, what it takes to rebuild, what matters when everything else is stripped away.

### The Reframing Practice

Reframing is not denial. It is not pretending painful things weren't painful. It is deliberately asking: *What did this experience give me that I couldn't have received any other way?*

That question repositions you from victim to student — from someone things happened *to*, to someone who grew from what happened. Both can be true at once. The pain was real. And so is the gift it left behind.

### Reflection Questions

1. What is the hardest thing you've been through? What specific understanding or capacity do you now carry because of it?
2. Name three people who most shaped you. What did each one give you — positively or negatively — that still shows up in who you are?
3. What does your background — family, culture, community, economic experience — uniquely equip you to understand or offer that others might not?

## Module 4: Abilities — Natural Gifts and Learned Skills

### Two Types of Ability

There is a critical distinction between **natural gifts** and **learned skills** — and understanding it helps you invest your development energy wisely.

**Natural gifts** are abilities that come without proportionate effort. They are disproportionately easy for you relative to most people. They may not feel remarkable precisely because they've always been effortless — but that effortlessness is the point. What is automatic for you is extraordinary to others.

**Learned skills** are abilities developed through deliberate practice, study, or experience. They may supplement your natural gifts, open doors your gifts alone can't open, or allow you to serve in fields adjacent to your core design.

Both matter. But natural gifts are your primary leverage points — the areas where your investment returns the highest output and where your best work lives.

### Finding Your Natural Gifts — Five Diagnostic Questions

1. **What comes almost embarrassingly easy to you?** Things that feel obvious but that others find complex or don't seem to think about at all.
2. **What do people specifically thank you for?** The things people seek you out for — without being prompted — point directly at your gifts.
3. **What do you pick up quickly?** Natural gifts accelerate the learning curve in specific domains.
4. **What energizes rather than depletes you?** Using a genuine natural gift tends to feel generative. You finish tired but satisfied, not empty.
5. **What did you do instinctively as a child, before you were trained?** Before formal education shaped your attention, what did you naturally gravitate toward?

### Domains of Ability

Research from Gallup's decades-long study of human performance identifies four broad domains in which human strengths cluster:

| Domain | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic Thinking** | Absorbing information, seeing patterns, making decisions | Analysis, ideation, systems thinking |
| **Relationship Building** | Connecting, understanding, and uniting people | Empathy, communication, trust-building |
| **Influencing** | Persuading, communicating, expanding ideas | Presenting, selling, advocating |
| **Executing** | Implementing, organizing, getting things done | Planning, discipline, follow-through |

You likely have natural strength in at least one or two of these domains — and they will feel like common sense to you, even when they seem mysterious to people with different wiring.

### The Strengths Principle

Marcus Buckingham and Donald Clifton, in their landmark book *Now, Discover Your Strengths*, made an observation that contradicts almost everything we're taught about development:

> *"Each person's greatest room for growth is in the areas of his or her greatest strength."*

We spend enormous energy trying to fix weaknesses. And there is a place for that — blind spots need to be managed. But the transformational investment — the one that produces world-class performance — is deepening your strengths, not patching your gaps.

Manage your weaknesses. Maximize your strengths. That is the path to irreplaceable contribution.

### Reflection Questions

1. What three abilities do people consistently compliment you on, even when you didn't try particularly hard?
2. In which of the four domains — Strategic Thinking, Relationship Building, Influencing, or Executing — do you feel most naturally at home?
3. What is one natural gift you have been underusing? What would it look like to invest in it deliberately?

## Module 5: Makeup — Understanding How You're Wired

### Personality Is a Map, Not a Box

Understanding your personality is one of the most powerful tools available for self-knowledge — and one of the most misused.

Personality frameworks are not labels to trap you. They are maps. A good map doesn't tell you where to go — it tells you where you are, so you can make better decisions about how to move forward.

The framework used here draws on the **Big Five** — the most empirically validated personality model in psychological research, used across decades, cultures, and contexts.

### The Big Five Dimensions

**1. Openness to Experience**
Your appetite for novelty, complexity, and intellectual exploration.

| High Openness | Low Openness |
|---|---|
| Curious, imaginative, drawn to ideas | Practical, concrete, prefers the familiar |
| Thrives with ambiguity | Thrives with structure and clarity |
| Generates creative options | Implements reliably |

**2. Conscientiousness**
Your capacity for discipline, organization, and goal-directed behavior.

| High Conscientiousness | Low Conscientiousness |
|---|---|
| Organized, reliable, self-disciplined | Flexible, spontaneous, adaptive |
| Finishes what they start | Responds well to changing conditions |
| Plans ahead | Thrives in the moment |

**3. Extraversion**
Your orientation toward the outer world — people, action, stimulation.

| High Extraversion | Low Extraversion (Introversion) |
|---|---|
| Energized by social interaction | Energized by solitude |
| Expressive, assertive, broad network | Reflective, depth-oriented, close relationships |
| Thinks out loud | Thinks to process, then speaks |

**4. Agreeableness**
Your orientation toward others — cooperation, empathy, and social harmony.

| High Agreeableness | Low Agreeableness |
|---|---|
| Warm, cooperative, attuned to others | Direct, objective, comfortable with conflict |
| Creates relational safety | Makes hard calls without hesitation |
| Builds cohesion | Drives accountability |

**5. Emotional Stability (vs. Neuroticism)**
Your tendency toward consistent emotional response versus volatility.

| High Stability | Lower Stability (High Reactivity) |
|---|---|
| Calm under pressure, resilient | Processes emotion deeply and intensely |
| Recovers quickly from setbacks | Highly attuned to emotional nuance |
| Consistent and predictable | Responsive to emotional environment |

### The Critical Principle

No personality profile is superior to another. Every combination carries genuine strengths and genuine blind spots.

The introvert who processes deeply brings something the extrovert doesn't. The highly agreeable person creates the relational safety that the direct person's accountability might fracture. The conscientious organizer executes what the highly open innovator imagines but can't always finish.

The world needs all of it.

What your Makeup tells you:
- **Where you naturally thrive** — environments and roles that fit your wiring
- **Where you need support** — areas where your natural style creates friction
- **How to communicate better** — with people whose Makeup is different from yours
- **How to protect your energy** — by working with your nature rather than constantly against it

### Reflection Questions

1. Based on the Big Five, what are your strongest dimensions? What are your weakest? Do these match what people who know you well would say?
2. What environments consistently energize you? What does that tell you about your wiring?
3. Think of someone whose personality is very different from yours. What do they bring to a team or room that you don't? What can you bring that they can't?

## Module 6: Expression — Your Unique Signature

### What Expression Is

Take two people with identical passion, identical background, identical abilities, and identical personality. They would still show up differently in the world.

Because Expression — the final dimension of FRAME — is the unique way you *deliver* everything else. It is your style, your tone, your method. The signature that appears on everything you do whether you intend it to or not.

Consider: countless musicians have played guitar. No one sounds exactly like Jimi Hendrix. Countless writers have written about justice. No one writes like James Baldwin. Countless scientists have studied the physical world. No one explained it like Richard Feynman. Same instrument, same subject — completely different signature.

That signature is not fabricated. It emerges naturally from the intersection of everything else in your FRAME. Expression is the fifth dimension because it builds on the other four. It is how your Fire, Roots, Abilities, and Makeup show up together as *you*.

### Finding Your Expression

Expression typically emerges at the intersection of three things:

**How you naturally communicate** — Do you explain through stories? Through data and logic? Through analogy and metaphor? Through demonstration? Through questions that make others think?

**What medium feels most alive** — Writing, speaking, building, designing, connecting, teaching, leading, creating, coding, organizing. Where does your work feel most like yourself?

**What others respond to** — When people say *"that was exactly what I needed"* — what was happening? What were you doing, and how were you doing it, in those moments?

### Authentic vs. Performed Expression

There is a difference between style that is performed and style that is authentic. Performed style is calibrated for effect — it's what you think people want. Authentic style emerges when you stop trying to be impressive and simply show up honestly.

The most effective leaders, communicators, and creators are almost always the most authentic — not because authenticity is a virtue (though it is), but because it is *efficient.* It doesn't cost energy to maintain. It doesn't create the cognitive load of performance. It frees you to focus on the work rather than the impression.

The paradox of Expression is that the more fully yourself you become, the more resonant and powerful your impact. The people who have the greatest influence are rarely the most polished. They are the most real.

### Reflection Questions

1. If three people who know you well described your "style" in two words each, what might they say?
2. What format or medium feels most natural when you want to express something important?
3. Describe a moment when you felt completely "on" — when what you were doing and who you were matched perfectly. What were you doing? What made that possible?

## Module 7: Reading Your Full FRAME Profile

### Synthesis — Putting It All Together

By now you've explored all five dimensions of your Frame. This final module helps you synthesize them into a coherent profile — a working map of how you are specifically designed to contribute.

This is not a final answer. It is a working draft, to be refined over years of living, building, and learning. Treat it as such.

### Your FRAME Profile Template

**F — Fire**
> My deepest passion is: ___________________
> The problems I feel called to address are: ___________________
> When I completely lose track of time, I am usually: ___________________

**R — Roots**
> The experiences that most shaped me are: ___________________
> What only my story could have taught me: ___________________
> The perspective I bring that others often don't have is: ___________________

**A — Abilities**
> My natural gifts are: ___________________
> My most valuable learned skills are: ___________________
> The domain where I operate at my best is: ___________________

**M — Makeup**
> My personality strengths are: ___________________
> The environments where I thrive: ___________________
> My blind spots or areas where I need support: ___________________

**E — Expression**
> My natural communication style is: ___________________
> The medium that feels most authentic to me: ___________________
> My signature — what makes my work recognizably mine — is: ___________________

### Using Your FRAME Profile

Your FRAME Profile is a decision-making instrument. Use it to:

**Evaluate opportunities** — Does this align with my Fire? Does it use my Abilities? Does it fit my Makeup?

**Find your role** — In a team, a project, or an organization, what does my FRAME bring that others don't?

**Guide your development** — Which element of my FRAME do I want to deepen most right now?

**Navigate your career** — Am I moving toward environments that match my Makeup? Toward work that uses my Abilities? Toward problems that ignite my Fire?

**Understand your relationships** — Where does my FRAME complement the people around me? Where does it create friction — and why?
